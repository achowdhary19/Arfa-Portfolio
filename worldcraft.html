About

In summer 2025, I lead a studio on game development for middle schoolers at NuVu Innovation School in Cambridge, MA. My goal was to create an approachable and empowering setting to introduce kids to programming and the maker journey . I adapted a curriculum, taught daily seminars, and guided students from initial concept to playable prototype through brainstorming, design, storytelling, world-building, and programming.

Teaching Approach

Building a project from concept to finish can feel intimidating, especially when the process is so often made to look clean and straightforward. I wanted students to approach new technologies with curiosity instead of fear — treating confusion and uncertainty as playful entry points rather than reasons to stop. The hands-on studio format, as opposed to a lecture-based classroom, let me watch each student work and meet them where they were. With a small, varied group, I built in multiple ways for them to engage, take in information, and show what they knew.


Beyond programming and design skills, I taught goal-setting, collaboration, reflection, and feedback. Above all, I wanted students to feel what it's like to experiment, mess up, and go back to the drawing board. This class was less about games or code, and more about learning to try things and test them. Using the syllabus as a guide rather than a ruleset, I worked with each student's individual strengths and pace, and made sure they left with real confidence navigating an unfamiliar, non-linear process — a foundation in design thinking and problem-solving that goes well beyond programming.

Studio Process

Each day paired a short lesson on a piece of the design process with hands-on practice. We opened by unpacking what makes games work — narrative, world design, gameplay — then moved into collaborative brainstorming, where students generated as many ideas as possible for environments, mechanics, and narratives under a time constraint. Students frankensteined their favorite ideas into one concept, storyboarded it, then moved into asset creation and digital prototyping using Delightex Edu (formerly CoSpaces Edu), a block-based 3D engine with AR/VR capabilities. The rest of the studio was spent building, debugging, and playtesting, ending with a final exposition where students presented their games and process to an audience.

Reflection

Teaching this course deepened my interest in designing tools and learning environments that turn complexity into something approachable — the same instinct that drives my design work outside the classroom. The most meaningful outcome was watching students build real confidence navigating an unfamiliar technology, which is exactly the kind of experience I want my work to create
